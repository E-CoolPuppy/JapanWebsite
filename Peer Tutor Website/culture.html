Etiquette

In Japan, people greet each other by bowing instead of a handshake. Generally, the lower
the bow, the greater the respect. For foreigners, a subtle dip of the head will be suitable as
a casual greeting to others.

When indoors, almost all places expect you to take off your shoes as courtesy. Shoes
should also be placed pointed towards the door, rather than into the building.

Tipping in Japan is not common. Generally, most restaurants, hotels, and taxi drivers do
not expect a tip, and may even refuse it out of courtesy. Do not be offended, as not
leaving a tip is not considered rude.

Before one eats, Japanese people will put their hands together and say “Itadakimasu” (いただきます),
which roughly translates to “I humbly receive”. It is a way of showing gratitude for the food given to you.
At the end of the meal, they will say “Gochisōsama deshita” (ごちそうさま　でした),
meaning “Thank you for the delicious meal”, again showing how thankful one is.
